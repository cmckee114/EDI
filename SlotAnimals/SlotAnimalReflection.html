The steps were as follows:

  • Brainstorm
  • Thinking of animals that we might want to take from 2D to 3D by making paper (and later wood) into slot animals
  • Prototyping in paper
  • We began thinking about ways to make our animals 3D by looking at images/ toy animals from different angles of the animal and cutting shapes that would fit together
  • Finalizing the paper prototype
  • When we found shapes that would fit together and looked like our animal, we refined the lines and slot locations until we got a final prototype
  • Capture
  • We took pictures of the pieces of paper from our final prototype against black paper with sharpie dots in the endpoints of the slots we made
  • Digitalization
  • We loaded the picture of our cut out paper prototype into Illustrator and created a new artboard layer and traced our animal shapes using the skills we learned in our pen tool assignment
  • We also created spaces for slots that were the width of the wood we were using so that the pieces fit together nicely when cut with the laser cutter
  • Tracing the Score/Raster
  • We then took a sheet of tracing paper and layed it over our printed file and traced out where we wanted lines/fill to show up on our slot animal in sharpie and then put this into our illustrator file and vectorized it
  • Layer organization
  • Once we had our shapes traced on illustrator we ordangized each shape into layers depending on if we wanted the line/area scored, rastered, or cut and assigned each layer to a color depending on which task we wanted the machine to perform (cut=red, raster=blue, score = black fill)
  • Paper Prototype Cut
  • We tested if the width of our slots were the proper width when we printed the cut version 1 of our illustrator file by trying to fit the width of the wood into our slots and if it fit we had a correctly allotted amount of space and if done correctly we loaded our V1 into the Final folder ready to be cut by the laser
  • Laser cutting/Putting it together
  • We then cut out our shapes using the laser cutter and we put the wood pieces together to form our slot animal!

The process:

Firstly, the parameters of this project were that we needed to include both raster and score elements and that the animal cutouts needed to fit within a 12 x 9 in piece of wood. I started my project by trying to figure out which animal I wanted to make. I thought that I wanted to make a shark but I wanted something that was much taller than that and I thought of my favorite animal which is the octopus because its legs are long and curly and strong. Then I started cutting pieces and made sure that there were 8 legs meaning I needed 4 legs per piece of paper if I had two pieces of paper. When I made my final paper prototype it was much cleaner than the first and I photographed it against black paper with sharpie points where the slots I made ended. I then loaded the picture of the cut out octopi into Illustrator and created a new artboard layer and traced our animal shapes using the skills I learned in the pen tool assignment and once done, I copied the single traced octopus to make two and then created separate slot rectangles so that they fit together when cut. I made sure to keep my layers in order by color and fill and once completely organized I saved this as my V1.

Challenges, setbacks, and solutions:
One challenge I found was when my computer shut down and did not save my original file with the digital octopus shapes and tracing paper layer. Although this was frustrating, I still had a printed picture of what my octopus shapes looked like so I was able to scan and vectorize those shapes in illustrator pretty quickly, recreating the slots, organizing my layers with the original tracing paper which I had saved and catching up to my classmates. Another challenge I had was trying to make sure that there were no double vectors when I had to redo my octopus shapes by taking pictures of my previous version. Once I finally got my octopus shape to look the way I liked, I just copied the first one into the second one and grouped the objects in the “cut” layer. Also, as you will see, I took a lot of process pictures to document my journey in this project!
